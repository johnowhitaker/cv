Consultant
Resource assessments, sustainability and research

Worked with Clare Griffiths on a series of projects for Phytotrade Africa and Bio-Innovation Zimbabwe (BIZ). Each project had it's own unique challenges, but most involved literature reviews, data collection, GIS work, some form of analysis and writing up reports or papers. The purpose of this research was to provide information to local businesses with the aim of developing indigenous plant species commercially and thus providing a sustainable source of income to some of Zimbabwe's most marginalized people. Read More

The first project involved conducting a Resource Survey of baobab trees in Zimbabwe. Estimating the total number of trees, the health of the population and the maximum sustainable fruit harvest. By supplementing our field work with data gleaned from satellite imagery and coming up with more accurate ways of predicting the tree density based on environmental conditions we were able to complete the project more quickly and cost effectively than would otherwise have been possible. Over the course of this project I built up a set of scripts for population distribution and density modelling (using python with machine learning libraries), geoprocessing and rapid manual surveying of satellite imagery that would come in useful for all the following projects. I also worked on using neural networks to count tree crowns within the satellite images, but within the scope of our project it was more accurate to simply perform the counting manually. During the course of this project, we also discovered the presence of a second species of baobab in Zimbabwe - a finding that turned into my first scientific publication, published in the South African Journal of Botany.

Following on from the baobab work, a simmilar assesment of the Marula tree population was conducted. Then we moved on to studying indigenous melons, sending off seed samples to be analysed for pharmaceutically interesting oils. We mapped the extent of the indigenous bamboo plantations and provided a booklet with allthe information needed to start a bamboo growing project with the goal of preventing the deforestation associated with the small scale tobacco industry. We travelled to Botswana to work with an organization harvesting a plant called Devil's Claw (Harphagophytum spp.) in partnership with the San community in Ghanzi District. Finally, I spent some time in Zimbabwe's Hwange province investigating the feasibility of establishing a buisness around pressing the oil from five different target species in the area.

This work was incredibly rewarding, with almost immediate action taken in response to our recommendations and a feeling of being involved in work that truly mattered. The job was always varied, encompassing everything from software development to field research, collaborating with the University of Zimbabwe one day to meeting with community leaders in a rural village the next, hiking through some of the most incredible landscapes collecting data and then spending days refining models on a computer.
